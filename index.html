Understanding Weather Outlooks

Plain-language guide to what each category means — no meteorology degree required.

The SPC Categorical Outlook tells you the overall risk of severe thunderstorms anywhere in a shaded area. "Severe" means hail ≥1 inch, wind gusts ≥58 mph, or a tornado.

Thunderstorm (TSTM)
Ordinary (non-severe) thunderstorms are possible. No significant risk of large hail, damaging winds, or tornadoes — but lightning and heavy rain can still occur. Think of a typical summer storm.
Marginal Risk (MRGL)
A low risk of isolated severe storms. Severe weather is possible but not expected to be widespread. Most people in the area will likely not see severe weather, but a quick spin-up tornado or a brief strong storm is worth watching.
Slight Risk (SLGT)
A few scattered severe storms are expected. You probably won't see severe weather at your exact location, but some areas nearby will. Stay weather-aware and have a plan to take shelter quickly. Hail, damaging winds, and an isolated tornado are all possible.
Enhanced Risk (ENH)
Numerous severe storms are likely. More areas will be impacted than in a Slight Risk. Expect a greater chance of large hail, widespread damaging wind gusts, and the possibility of a few tornadoes. Take this seriously and know your shelter options in advance.
Moderate Risk (MDT)
A significant severe weather outbreak is expected. Widespread damaging storms, large hail, and multiple tornadoes — including possibly strong (EF2+) ones — are likely over a large area. Serious preparations are needed. This is a high-impact weather day.
High Risk (HIGH)
An extremely dangerous, potentially historic severe weather event is expected. Rare — issued only a few times per year at most. A major tornado outbreak with violent (EF4–EF5) tornadoes, extreme hail, or a destructive wind event is likely. Take immediate action; treat this as a life-threatening situation.

The Probability Outlook shows the chance (within 25 miles of any point) that a specific hazard — tornado, damaging wind, or large hail — will occur. Higher percentages mean a higher chance of that specific hazard hitting your area.

2% — Very Low Chance
A very small chance that a tornado/large hail/damaging wind occurs within 25 miles of you. Think of this as "keep an eye on the weather" territory — most people will not be affected.
5% — Low Chance
Still a relatively small chance, but worth being aware. Isolated severe weather is possible. Have a plan ready but don't cancel outdoor activities just yet.
10% — Elevated Chance
A meaningful chance of severe weather near you. Scattered storms capable of this hazard are expected. Stay weather-aware throughout the day and know where to seek shelter.
15% — Moderate Chance
A solid chance that this specific hazard affects your area. Prepare ahead of time, monitor weather updates frequently, and be ready to act quickly.
30% — High Chance
A significant chance of this hazard occurring. Multiple events are expected. This is a high-end severe weather day for this hazard type. Have shelter plans finalized and be ready to act at a moment's notice.
45%+ — Very High Chance
A very high likelihood of this hazard within 25 miles. A major outbreak is expected. Take this extremely seriously — prepare now, not later.
60%+ — Extremely High
Extremely rare. A catastrophic, near-certain outbreak for this hazard. Used for the most dangerous outbreak days. All residents in this area should take shelter well before storms arrive.
Conditional Moderate (CIG1) — All Hazards
This is a level 1/3 on the CIG system. This is used for all outlooks, and each one means something. A tornado CIG1 means that a strong (EF2) tornado is possible in the area. For wind, it represents that an all hazards event is possible, with winds gusts up to 65kts (74mph). For hail, it represents the possibility of tennis ball to baseball sized hail (2"-3.5").
Conditional Moderate (CIG2) — All Hazards
This is a level 2/3 on the CIG system. This is used for all outlooks, and each one means something. A tornado CIG2 means that a intense (EF3) tornado is possible in the area. For wind, it means that a derecho, or a long lasting storm producing damaging winds throughout is possible. For hail, it represents the possibility of softball sized hail (3.5") or higher.
Conditional High (CIG3) — Tornado & Wind Only
This is a level 3/3 on the CIG system. This is used for only Tornado and Wind Outlooks, and each one means something. A tornado CIG3 means that a violent (EF4+) tornado is possible in the area. For wind, it means that a high end derecho, or a long lasting storm producing damaging winds and some destructive winds (80mph) throughout is actively occurring.

The WPC Excessive Rainfall Outlook shows where heavy rain could cause flash flooding. It reflects the probability of rainfall that exceeds flash flood guidance thresholds — not just "heavy rain," but rain heavy enough to cause flooding of roads, streams, and low-lying areas.

Marginal Risk
Isolated flash flooding is possible, mainly in spots with poor drainage, areas that already had recent rain, or burn scars. Most people won't experience flooding, but stay aware if you're near a stream or in a low-lying area.
Slight Risk
Scattered flash flooding is expected somewhere in the area. Some roads and streams may flood. Avoid driving through flooded roadways and monitor local weather alerts. Vulnerable areas like underpasses are especially at risk.
Moderate Risk
Widespread flash flooding is likely across a large area. Significant impacts expected — multiple roads flooded, streams and rivers rising rapidly, and potential for structural flooding. Be prepared to move to higher ground and avoid all flood-prone areas.
High Risk
Major, potentially life-threatening flash flooding is expected. Rare. Rivers could rise dramatically and rapidly. This is a flood emergency-level event — do not attempt to travel through any flooded area. Have an evacuation plan ready.

The SPC Fire Weather Outlook highlights areas where dry air, strong winds, and low humidity create dangerous conditions for wildfires to ignite and spread rapidly. Even a small spark from a campfire, downed power line, or cigarette can become a fast-moving wildfire.

Elevated
Conditions are somewhat favorable for fire spread. Grasses and brush can ignite more easily than usual and fires may spread faster than normal. Use extra caution with any open flames outdoors.
Critical
Fire weather conditions are dangerous. A combination of very low relative humidity (often below 15–20%), strong winds, and dry fuels creates ideal wildfire conditions. Any fire that starts could spread rapidly and be difficult to control. Outdoor burning is extremely hazardous.
Extremely Critical
Historically dangerous fire weather conditions are expected. Extreme wind speeds combined with very low humidity and critically dry fuels create a recipe for explosive, potentially uncontrollable wildfires. Evacuations may be necessary. This is a rare, high-impact event — used for the most dangerous fire weather setups of the year.
Remember: Fire weather outlooks focus on the atmosphere's potential to support fire spread — local fuel conditions, recent precipitation, and any active fire situations also play a key role in actual fire behavior.

Weather Data Sources

NWS Default

National Weather Service — official U.S. government weather agency. Data comes directly from NOAA observation stations and forecast offices.

  • Official government forecasts — highly reliable for U.S.
  • Real-time station observations (temperature, pressure, clouds)
  • Wind data supplemented by Open-Meteo for better accuracy
  • UV index supplemented by Open-Meteo (NWS doesn't provide it)
  • Uses huamn forecasts instead of single model outputs without human input
Google

Google Weather API — Google's weather service, which aggregates data from multiple providers to produce smoothed, model-based forecasts worldwide.

  • Good global coverage, works for any location worldwide
  • Provides full 10-day forecasts
  • May differ from NWS, especially for temperature and precipitation timing
  • Smoothed/blended data — can sometimes feel less "local"
  • Uses a ensemble (combination of multiple models to create one model output) of multiple google weather models. Still less accurate than NWS forecasts
Open-Meteo

Open-Meteo — free, open-source weather API based on European and global numerical weather models (ERA5, GFS, ECMWF).

  • Uses long range global models (ECMWF, GFS) — strong for medium-range forecasts
  • Up to 16-day forecast available
  • Can differ from NWS on short-term timing of precipitation
  • Excellent wind and UV index data from model output
  • Uses a mix of models, which is better than a single model output, but still isn't as accurate as NWS human forecasts
OWM

OpenWeatherMap — a popular weather data provider with global coverage, using a blend of weather station data and numerical models.

  • Good global coverage with hyperlocal station blending
  • Free tier provides current conditions + 5-day/3-hour forecast
  • Daily forecast is derived by aggregating 3-hour intervals
  • UV index not included in free tier (supplemented by Open-Meteo)
  • Also powers the temperature, wind, and cloud map overlays
  • Only uses a single model output, which is less accurate compared to NWS human forecasts
Tip: Differences between sources are normal — each uses different models, observation networks, and update schedules. NWS is generally the best choice for U.S. locations. Open-Meteo and OWM are useful for comparing forecasts or when NWS coverage isn't available.
